# **The North American Mobile App Economy in 2025: A Comparative Analysis of the Gaming and Fitness Markets**

## **Executive Summary**

This report provides a comprehensive market analysis of the North American Mobile Gaming and Mobile Fitness App sectors, with a specific focus on the United States and Canada for the year 2025\. The analysis synthesizes data from leading industry sources to quantify market size, deconstruct user demographics and behaviors, and identify the core drivers and strategic imperatives shaping these two pivotal segments of the digital economy.

The North American mobile landscape in 2025 is defined by two industries at vastly different stages of maturity and scale. The **Mobile Gaming market** stands as a mature, highly lucrative colossus, a dominant force in the entertainment sector. Navigating conflicting data from various sources, which measure different aspects of the value chain (from gross consumer spend to net publisher revenue), this report provides a synthesized 2025 market valuation for North American mobile gaming in the range of **$50 billion to $60 billion**. This valuation is anchored by an exceptionally high average revenue per paying user and sophisticated, event-driven monetization models.

In stark contrast, the **Mobile Fitness App market** is a smaller but dynamic high-growth sector. Its 2025 North American market size is estimated to be between **$5.5 billion and $6.5 billion**. The industry's expansion is inextricably linked to the proliferation of wearable technology and a broader societal shift towards digital wellness solutions. However, its growth trajectory is constrained by a critical, unresolved structural challenge: extremely high user churn. While user acquisition is strong, long-term retention remains the industry's primary obstacle.

The core distinction between these markets lies in their fundamental business models and user value propositions. Mobile gaming thrives on a free-to-play, in-app purchase (IAP) model that excels at creating high-engagement loops and converting a fraction of its user base into high-value spenders. Mobile fitness, conversely, relies predominantly on a recurring subscription model that struggles to align with the often-cyclical nature of user motivation, resulting in significant subscriber churn.

Moving forward, both sectors face a strategic inflection point where the focus must shift from pure user acquisition to maximizing the lifetime value of existing customers. For mobile gaming, this involves deepening engagement through more complex, "core" experiences and innovating in saturated genres. For mobile fitness, the imperative is to solve the retention crisis by adopting proven gamification mechanics and evolving from simple content libraries into indispensable data hubs for a user's entire wellness ecosystem. This report deconstructs these dynamics to provide a clear, data-driven perspective on the opportunities and challenges within each market for the 2025-2030 forecast period.

## **Section 1: The North American Mobile Gaming Colossus: Market Scale and Dynamics**

The mobile gaming market in North America is not merely a segment of the app economy; it is a cultural and economic powerhouse that rivals, and in many cases surpasses, traditional entertainment industries like film and music.1 Characterized by its immense scale, deep user engagement, and unparalleled monetization efficiency, this market represents the most lucrative region globally on a per-player basis. This section deconstructs the market's valuation, analyzes its high-value player base, and provides a granular breakdown of the competitive dynamics at the genre level.

### **1.1 Market Valuation and Forecasts: Navigating the Data Discrepancies**

Quantifying the precise size of the North American mobile gaming market is a complex task, with leading market intelligence firms presenting figures that vary significantly. This variance is not necessarily indicative of error but rather reflects different measurement methodologies, primarily the distinction between gross consumer spending at the app store level and the net revenue received by publishers. A comprehensive understanding requires analyzing these different data streams to arrive at a synthesized, holistic view of the market's true economic footprint.

One of the most direct measures of market activity comes from transaction tracking firms. SensorTower, for instance, reports that consumer spending on mobile game in-app purchases (IAP) in the **United States alone reached an astounding $52 billion in 2024**.2 This figure represents the gross revenue flowing through app stores before platform holders like Apple and Google take their commission (typically 15-30%).

In contrast, other sources provide figures that appear more conservative but may reflect a different scope. Grand View Research estimates the entire North American mobile gaming market was valued at **$32.06 billion in 2024**, with a projected compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 9% from 2025 to 2030\.3 This lower figure could represent publisher net revenue after platform fees and other deductions have been accounted for. The same source notes that North America accounted for 23.0% of the global mobile gaming market in 2024\.3

Further context is provided by Newzoo, a premier source for games industry intelligence. Newzoo's 2025 forecast places the total North American *gaming* market (encompassing mobile, console, and PC) at **$52.7 billion**.4 Globally, Newzoo projects the mobile gaming segment will generate **$103.0 billion** in 2025, representing a commanding 55% of the total global games market of $188.8 billion.7 Critically, Newzoo also specifies that the US and China together will account for half of all global consumer games spending in 2025, with the **US market forecast at $49.6 billion**.5 This US-specific figure from Newzoo aligns closely with SensorTower's 2024 IAP data, suggesting that the true consumer-facing market size is indeed in the tens of billions for the US alone.

The logical conclusion from this data is that the definition of "market size" is the key variable. The gross value of consumer transactions is the most accurate reflection of the market's economic scale from a user perspective. Therefore, the higher-end estimates provide a more strategically relevant picture.

### **1.2 The Player Landscape: Demographics and High-Value Engagement**

The foundation of North America's mobile gaming dominance is its large, diverse, and highly engaged player base. The region comprises an estimated **285 million total gamers**, with the United States accounting for **214.5 million** and Canada contributing **22.8 million**.10 Globally, mobile is the undisputed leading platform, attracting 3.0 billion of the world's 3.58 billion players in 2025, a share of approximately 83%.7

The defining feature of the North American player is not just participation but a profound willingness to spend. The average annual spend per *paying* gamer in North America is **$325**, a figure that starkly illustrates the region's monetization power.4 This is nearly three times the average spend in Europe ($125) and more than three times the average across Eastern markets ($51.60).4 This high average revenue per paying user (ARPPU) is the central pillar supporting the market's multi-billion-dollar valuation.

Understanding the motivations behind this spending is critical for any market participant. Analysis shows that North American players are primarily driven by a desire to acquire **exclusive content (34%)** and to spend on **personalization and character customization (29%)**.4 This indicates a value perception tied to uniqueness, status, and enhanced gameplay experiences rather than pure price advantage. This contrasts with European players, who are more strongly motivated by deals, offers, and ad-free experiences.4 This cultural distinction has direct implications for monetization strategy, suggesting that models based on offering high-value, exclusive digital goods and cosmetic items will outperform discount-driven approaches in North America.

The demographic profile of the mobile gamer has also evolved significantly, shedding outdated stereotypes. Mobile gaming is a mainstream activity that spans all age groups. In the United States, a significant **33% of all mobile gamers are aged 35 and above**, demonstrating strong adoption among older, financially established demographics.11 Core cohorts like Gen Z and Millennials now spend more of their entertainment time on gaming than on any other medium.2

This broad adoption has rendered the traditional "gamer" identity largely obsolete for the mobile sector. A remarkable **85% of mobile players do not self-identify as "gamers"**.2 This is a crucial realization: mobile gaming is not a niche hobby but a form of mass-market entertainment, analogous to using social media or watching streaming video. Marketing and user acquisition efforts that narrowly target traditional "gamer" channels are therefore destined to miss the vast majority of the addressable market. The true target audience is the general mobile user, and strategies must be calibrated to this broad, mainstream reality.

### **1.3 Revenue Deep Dive: Deconstructing the US Market by Genre (H1 2025\)**

A granular analysis of the United States market for the first half of 2025 reveals a dynamic and shifting competitive landscape at the genre level. The data, which covers gross player spending through the App Store and Google Play, shows a market dominated by three heavyweight genres that collectively generated **$9.4 billion in just six months**.12 However, their individual trajectories reveal critical underlying market trends.

The top three genres by revenue in H1 2025 were:

1. **Puzzle:** Reclaiming the top position, the Puzzle genre generated **$3.5 billion**. It demonstrated healthy and sustained growth of 11% year-over-year (YoY), driven by enduring mega-franchises like *Royal Match* and *Candy Crush Saga*.12  
2. **Casino:** After a long period of dominance, the Casino genre slipped to second place, generating **$3.0 billion**. This figure represents a sharp and significant **25% YoY decline** from the $4.0 billion it earned in H1 2024, signaling potential market saturation and player fatigue.12  
3. **Strategy:** Rounding out the top three, the Strategy genre generated **$2.9 billion**. Crucially, it exhibited the strongest growth among the market leaders, with revenue **surging by 28% YoY**. This growth was fueled by titles like *Last War: Survival* and *Whiteout Survival*.12

While smaller in absolute terms, other genres also displayed notable trends. The **Arcade** genre, though generating a more modest $287.2 million, posted the highest YoY growth rate in the US at **52%**, indicating an appetite for innovative, "snackable" gameplay.12 Conversely, the **Simulation** genre, while still a billion-dollar category with $1.1 billion in H1 revenue, was one of the worst performers, contracting by 11% YoY.12

### **1.4 The Canadian Mobile Gaming Market: A Distinct Entity**

While often grouped with the US, the Canadian mobile gaming market possesses its own distinct scale, growth drivers, and regulatory environment. Analysis of the Canadian market reveals a robust and rapidly expanding sector where mobile is the clear engine of growth.

As with the broader North American market, valuation figures for Canada vary. Grand View Research reports the total Canadian gaming market (all platforms) at **$11.69 billion in 2024**, with a strong projected CAGR of 9.1% from 2025 to 2030\.13 A separate report from IMARC Group provides a more conservative 2024 valuation of **$4.85 billion**, with an 8.36% CAGR forecast through 2033\.14

Regardless of the top-line figure, both sources agree on a critical point: **mobile is the largest and fastest-growing segment** within the Canadian market. It accounted for **52.98% of total gaming revenue in 2024**.13 Applying this share to the higher valuation from Grand View Research suggests a 2024 Canadian mobile gaming market size of approximately **$6.2 billion**.

The user base in Canada is substantial, with an estimated **22.8 million gamers** in total.10 The number of mobile-specific players is projected to climb to **13.67 million by 2027**, indicating significant headroom for further growth.14

A key differentiator and primary growth catalyst in Canada is the evolving regulatory landscape for online gambling, or iGaming. The regulated mobile iGaming market in Canada is projected to generate approximately **$1.96 billion by 2025**.15 This rapid expansion is being driven by top-down legislative changes, most notably the establishment of a competitive online gambling market in Ontario. Data from Q2 2024-25 shows that Ontario's mobile gambling market generated CA$22.7 billion in total wagers, with nearly one million active player accounts.15 This demonstrates that, unlike in the US where growth is primarily driven by organic consumer trends in traditional IAP-based games, a significant portion of Canada's market expansion is being fueled by the opening of new, highly lucrative, real-money gaming segments. The future growth trajectory of the Canadian market will therefore be heavily influenced by the pace and scope of similar iGaming regulation in other provinces.

## **Section 2: The Digital Wellness Boom: The North American Mobile Fitness App Market**

The North American mobile fitness app market represents a dynamic and high-growth sector at the confluence of technology, healthcare, and evolving consumer lifestyles. Propelled by the ubiquity of smartphones and the rise of wearable technology, these applications have become central to how millions of consumers manage their health and wellness. This section will analyze the market's valuation, dissect its user base and the critical challenge of retention, and explore the symbiotic relationship with hardware that defines its ecosystem.

### **2.1 Market Valuation and Forecasts**

Estimates for the size of the North American mobile fitness app market vary considerably, reflecting different definitions of what the "market" encompasses—from pure app store revenue to the broader economic activity enabled by these platforms.

Grand View Research provides one of the most comprehensive valuations, estimating the North American market at **$4.94 billion in 2024**, with a projected CAGR of 12.2% from 2025 to 2030\.16 This source places the global market at $10.59 billion in 2024, rising to **$12.12 billion in 2025**.17 North America's dominant position is clear, accounting for a substantial **46.6% share of the global market** in 2024\.16

Other sources offer more conservative figures. Straits Research, for example, values the global market at just $2.10 billion in 2024, projected to reach **$2.47 billion in 2025**.19 If North America holds its 42.5% share, this would imply a regional market of only \~$1.05 billion in 2025\. At the higher end of the spectrum, a report from Technavio forecasts that the global market will add $101.60 billion in value between 2025 and 2029, with North America contributing 31% of that growth, suggesting a very large and rapidly expanding market.20

The significant gap between these valuations suggests that the fitness app market's true value lies in its role as an **ecosystem enabler**. The lower estimates likely focus narrowly on revenue generated directly within app stores (IAP and paid downloads). The higher, more plausible valuations almost certainly account for the broader revenue streams that are intrinsically linked to the app experience. This includes high-margin subscriptions for connected fitness equipment (e.g., Peloton), premium features bundled with wearables (e.g., Fitbit Premium), and other services where the app serves as the essential software interface. The app is the low-cost entry point to a much larger, higher-value digital wellness ecosystem.

For the Canadian market specifically, the 2024 valuation is estimated between **$612.1 million** 21 and **$750 million**.22 The market is projected to experience robust growth, with CAGR forecasts ranging from 11.57% to 16.9%.21

### **2.2 The User Base: Adoption, Demographics, and the Retention Challenge**

The mobile fitness app market has achieved widespread adoption across North America, but this success in user acquisition is deeply undermined by a persistent and severe challenge in long-term user retention.

In the United States, market penetration is remarkably high, with over **74% of Americans reporting use of at least one fitness app**.23 The serviceable obtainable market (SOM) in the U.S. is on a trajectory to reach **150 million users by 2027**, indicating a massive and engaged audience.23 In Canada, user penetration is also significant and growing, expected to reach **29.96% in 2025**.21

The demographic profile of the fitness app user is concentrated among younger, digitally native cohorts. **Millennials constitute the largest audience segment (60%)**, followed by **Gen Z (25%)**.23 Usage is generally higher among women, who also tend to have longer average session times than their male counterparts.2 In Canada, the 25-34 age group exhibits the highest levels of engagement.21

Despite this successful acquisition of a broad user base, the industry is plagued by what can only be described as a **retention crisis**. This is the single greatest structural weakness of the mobile fitness market. Data from the Canadian market is particularly stark, revealing that an astonishing **80% of users abandon fitness apps within the first six months** of downloading them.21 Broader benchmarks confirm this trend, with the average 30-day retention rate for fitness apps hovering at a mere 27.2%.18 While top-performing applications can achieve a more respectable 47.5% retention, the industry average points to a fundamental problem.18

This combination of high penetration and extremely low retention creates a "leaky bucket" business model. The industry is highly effective at attracting new users, particularly around seasonal peaks like New Year's, but it fails to deliver sustained value for the majority of them, leading to massive, rapid churn. This means the lifetime value (LTV) of the average user is severely constrained, and companies must constantly spend on marketing and acquisition simply to replace the users who have left.

This phenomenon suggests a fundamental mismatch between the industry's dominant business model and the psychology of its user base. Most fitness apps rely on a recurring subscription model, which requires consistent, long-term usage to justify the ongoing cost. However, for many individuals, motivation for fitness is cyclical, intermittent, and often tied to specific short-term goals. The high churn rate indicates that for a large portion of the user base, the perceived value of the app does not consistently outweigh its recurring cost, leading them to cancel their subscriptions once their initial motivation wanes. This points to a significant opportunity for innovation in business models that better align with the fluctuating nature of user engagement.

### **2.3 Core Segments and Technology Integration**

The North American mobile fitness market is composed of several key segments, each serving different user needs. The **Exercise & Weight Loss** segment is the largest, commanding a 65.81% share of the market value in Canada, for example.21 However, the **Activity Tracking** segment is identified as the fastest-growing category, a trend that highlights a crucial market shift.16

The growth of the entire fitness app market is inextricably linked to the parallel explosion of the wearable technology market. This symbiotic relationship is fundamental to the industry's value proposition. In Canada, approximately one-third of adults (35% of women and 34% of men) already own a wearable device.21 The global wearable technology market is a massive industry in its own right, projected to reach **$62.82 billion by 2025**.18

Wearables—such as smartwatches and fitness trackers—are the primary source of the rich, passive data streams (e.g., step count, heart rate, sleep patterns) that power the core features of modern fitness apps. This integration allows apps to move beyond generic content and offer personalized insights, progress tracking, and adaptive coaching.

The dominant monetization model across all segments is the **subscription**, or "freemium," model. A basic version of the app is offered for free, with advanced features, personalized plans, and premium content locked behind a recurring payment. Typical price points for these premium subscriptions range from **$9.99 to $29.99 per month**.23 This model is highly lucrative when it works, with paid apps and subscriptions accounting for an estimated **60% of total market revenue**.23

The rapid growth of the Activity Tracking segment, combined with the deep integration with wearables, signals a critical evolution in the market's center of gravity. The future of fitness apps appears to lie less in being a simple "content library" of pre-recorded workout videos and more in becoming a sophisticated **"data hub."** The most defensible and valuable position in the market is to be the central platform that aggregates, analyzes, and interprets a user's health data from a multitude of sources—wearables, connected scales, smart gym equipment, and manual user input. By becoming the indispensable repository of a user's entire health history, these apps can create a powerful, sticky ecosystem that is difficult for users to leave, thereby directly addressing the industry's core retention problem.

### **2.4 The Canadian Market: A Case Study in Regional Adaptation**

The Canadian fitness app market serves as an excellent case study on the critical importance of regional adaptation. While part of the broader North American landscape, Canadian users exhibit unique behaviors and preferences that necessitate a localized product strategy. A generic, US-centric approach is likely to underperform in Canada.

Several key factors distinguish the Canadian market:

* **Seasonality:** Canadian app usage is highly influenced by the country's climate. Apps that provide a strong library of indoor workout alternatives see **45% higher usage during the long winter months**, a crucial consideration for content programming and user engagement strategies.21  
* **Bilingualism:** Language is a major factor in user engagement. In the province of Quebec, users show **28% higher engagement with applications that offer full, high-quality French language support**. This goes beyond simple translation to include culturally relevant content and marketing.21  
* **Infrastructure and Lifestyle:** The Canadian lifestyle, which often includes summer retreats to "cottage country" where internet connectivity can be unreliable, makes **offline functionality a key feature**. Apps that allow users to download workouts and access features without a live connection provide a significant practical advantage.21  
* **Rural Adoption:** Unlike in many other markets where digital services are concentrated in urban centers, rural residents in Canada are **37% more likely to use digital fitness solutions** than to join a physical gym.21 This highlights a key and potentially underserved growth demographic for fitness app providers.

These data points provide a clear roadmap for success in the Canadian market. They demonstrate that hyper-localization is a powerful competitive differentiator. Companies that invest in a dedicated Canadian product strategy—one that accounts for seasonal content, provides robust bilingual support, and ensures offline functionality—will be positioned to capture significantly higher engagement and market share than competitors who treat Canada as a mere extension of the US market. This underscores a broader global trend where deep cultural and environmental integration is becoming just as important as technical excellence.

## **Section 3: Strategic Analysis and Future Outlook**

A comparative analysis of the North American Mobile Gaming and Mobile Fitness App markets reveals two industries with fundamentally different economic structures, user relationships, and strategic priorities. While both operate within the same mobile ecosystem, their paths to monetization and their core challenges are distinct. Understanding these differences is essential for identifying future growth opportunities and navigating the evolving digital landscape.

### **3.1 Comparative Analysis: Monetization Models and User Value**

The most profound difference between the mobile gaming and fitness markets lies in their core business models. These models shape everything from product design to user retention and long-term profitability.

**Mobile Gaming** is overwhelmingly dominated by a sophisticated **free-to-play (F2P) model monetized through in-app purchases (IAP)**.11 This model excels at acquiring a massive user base at low friction and then converting a small percentage of highly engaged players into high-value spenders, colloquially known as "whales." Revenue generation is often event-driven, with developers using "live operations" (live-ops)—such as limited-time events, special offers, and new content releases—to create a sense of urgency and stimulate spending. The result is an extremely high average revenue per *paying* user, which in North America stands at an annualized figure of **$325**.4 The best-performing games achieve high retention rates among their paying user base, creating a predictable and highly profitable long-term revenue stream.

**Mobile Fitness**, in contrast, relies almost exclusively on a **recurring subscription model**.23 The strategic goal is to become an indispensable daily utility, a habit for which the user is willing to pay a recurring monthly or annual fee. Unlike gaming, revenue is spread more evenly across the entire paying user base rather than being concentrated among a few super-users. However, as previously detailed, this model is fundamentally challenged by extremely high user churn.21 The average revenue per user (ARPU) is consequently much lower. In Canada, for example, the projected ARPU of $25.22 translates to just over $300 annually, but this figure applies to the entire user base, not just paying subscribers, and is heavily diluted by the high churn rate.21

### **3.2 Emerging Trends Shaping the 2025-2030 Landscape**

Several macro trends are set to influence the trajectory of both markets in the coming years.

* **The Strategic Shift from Acquisition to Retention:** In mature markets like North America, user growth is flattening.25 This is forcing a critical strategic pivot across both industries. The era of growth-at-all-costs, fueled by massive user acquisition spending, is giving way to a focus on maximizing the lifetime value (LTV) of existing users. For gaming, this means deeper live-ops and community management. For fitness, it means solving the core retention problem is now an existential priority.4  
* **The Pervasive Integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI):** AI is no longer a futuristic concept but a core enabling technology. In **gaming**, AI is being used to accelerate development through procedural content generation, art assistance, and automated code creation.1 In **fitness**, AI is the engine driving the next wave of product innovation. It powers hyper-personalized coaching, uses data to detect workout plateaus, and provides smart, adaptive recommendations for nutrition and recovery, making the app a true digital coach.21  
* **Platform Fragmentation and Direct-to-Consumer Models:** The historic duopoly of the Apple App Store and Google Play is facing increasing challenges. In gaming, there is a growing trend towards direct-to-consumer monetization through web shops and alternative app stores, allowing publishers to bypass platform fees and own the customer relationship directly.7 While less pronounced in the fitness sector, this trend towards platform diversification represents a potential future avenue for growth and margin improvement.

### **3.3 Recommendations and Strategic Imperatives**

Based on the comprehensive analysis of these two markets, the following strategic recommendations are proposed for key stakeholders.

#### **For Mobile Gaming Stakeholders:**

1. **Double Down on "Core-ification":** The data clearly shows that revenue growth in the lucrative US market is being driven by genres with strategic depth, such as Strategy and RPGs. Investment in developing and acquiring high-quality games in these categories is a direct path to capturing the market's highest-spending players.  
2. **Innovate in Saturated Genres through Hybridization:** For hyper-competitive genres like Puzzle and Casino, differentiation is key. Success will come from innovating on core mechanics by blending them with other genres, such as the narrative and meta-progression systems seen in "merge & story" puzzle games, to create fresh experiences that can stand out.12  
3. **Optimize Monetization for North American Spending Behaviors:** Recognize that the primary motivation for spending in North America is the desire for exclusivity and customization. Monetization strategies should be built around high-value, desirable virtual goods, battle passes, and cosmetic items rather than relying on frequent, deep discounting, which can devalue the in-game economy.4

#### **For Mobile Fitness Stakeholders:**

1. **Solve the Retention Crisis with Proven Gamification:** The fitness industry must look to the gaming industry for proven solutions to its retention problem. The 80% six-month churn rate is unsustainable.21 Implementing core gamification loops—such as daily quests (e.g., "walk 5,000 steps"), progressive achievement systems, social leaderboards, and meaningful reward schedules—is the most critical strategic imperative to encourage habit formation and combat churn.  
2. **Embrace the "Data Hub" Model:** The long-term defensible strategy is to shift focus from being a simple content provider to becoming the indispensable central hub for a user's entire wellness data ecosystem. Deep integration with the full spectrum of wearables and connected health devices will create a sticky product that houses a user's health history, making it difficult to abandon.  
3. **Experiment with Monetization Beyond the Subscription:** The standard monthly subscription is a poor fit for the cyclical motivation of many users. The industry must experiment with more flexible models. This could include tiered access, one-time payments for specific training programs or challenges, a pay-per-use model for virtual classes, or even innovative models like "Move-to-Earn" that reward consistent engagement.28  
4. **Invest in Hyper-Localization for Key Markets:** As the Canadian case study demonstrates, regional adaptation is a powerful driver of engagement. For key international markets, investment in a localized product roadmap that addresses unique cultural, linguistic, and environmental factors—such as bilingual support and seasonal content in Canada—will yield a significant competitive advantage.21
