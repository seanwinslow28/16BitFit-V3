# **State of Monetization 2025: Benchmarks and Strategies for Mobile Gaming and Fitness Apps in North America**

## **Section 1: The North American Mobile App Economy in 2025: A Market Overview**

### **1.1 Executive Summary of the Macro Environment**

The North American mobile application economy is demonstrating a period of robust and sustained growth in the 2024-2025 period, having successfully navigated the market corrections of the post-pandemic era. This resurgence is built upon two powerful, parallel pillars: escalating consumer spending and unprecedented investment in mobile advertising. Following a dip in 2022, the global app economy has shown significant recovery, with worldwide consumer spending on mobile apps rising by 15.7% year-over-year in 2024\.1 This trend is mirrored in North America, which continues to be one of the most lucrative regions for app developers. The regional gaming market alone is projected to reach a valuation of USD 74.40 billion in 2025, underscoring the immense commercial scale.2 Similarly, the fitness app vertical shows remarkable strength, with the U.S. market projected to generate $2.49 billion in 2025\.3

Fueling this growth is a massive influx of capital into mobile advertising. In the United States, mobile ad spend is forecast to reach $228.11 billion in 2025, a substantial increase from $202 billion in 2024\. This figure represents a commanding 66.4% of the total digital advertising budget, firmly establishing mobile as the primary channel for marketers.1 This advertising investment directly supports the freemium and ad-supported monetization models that dominate the app landscape. Concurrently, user engagement remains exceptionally high, with smartphone users now spending an average of five hours per day on their devices.1 This combination of high engagement, strong consumer spending habits, and massive advertising investment creates a fertile ground for monetization. However, this optimistic landscape is tempered by significant underlying complexities, particularly in the realms of user acquisition, privacy, and measurement, which demand increasingly sophisticated strategies from developers and marketers to achieve sustainable growth.

### **1.2 The Enduring Impact of Platform Dynamics and Privacy Regulations**

The strategic landscape for mobile app monetization in North America is fundamentally shaped by two interconnected forces: the persistent behavioral differences between users on iOS and Android platforms, and the ongoing market adaptation to a privacy-centric ecosystem, primarily driven by Apple's App Tracking Transparency (ATT) framework. These factors create distinct operational realities and strategic imperatives for developers depending on their target platform and monetization model.

A clear bifurcation exists in user spending behavior. Apple's App Store consistently generates more revenue from in-app purchases and subscriptions, despite Android's larger global market share.5 This is largely attributed to the demographic and economic profile of the average iOS user, who exhibits a higher willingness to spend directly on digital content and services.5 Consequently, iOS remains the priority platform for developers focused on premium, IAP-driven, and subscription-based revenue models. In contrast, the sheer scale of the Android user base, which holds over 71% of the global smartphone market share, makes it the dominant platform for ad-based monetization and achieving mass-market reach through high download volumes.5

This platform dynamic is further complicated by the evolution of privacy standards. Apple's ATT framework, introduced in 2021, continues to be a defining operational challenge, limiting the availability of user-level data for ad targeting and measurement. However, the industry is showing signs of maturation and adaptation. In 2024, global ATT opt-in rates for iOS users who are shown the tracking prompt continued a slow but steady climb, rising from an average of 32% to 35% by the first quarter of 2025\.1 The Gaming category has maintained a relatively high opt-in rate of 39%, suggesting that engaged players are more willing to consent to tracking in exchange for a more personalized experience.1 This gradual increase in opt-ins, coupled with significant industry investment in privacy-first technologies and measurement solutions like SKAdNetwork (SKAN), indicates a strategic shift away from resisting the new paradigm and toward mastering it.1 For 2025, success is less about circumventing privacy rules and more about building trust with users and leveraging new analytical tools to drive growth in a data-constrained environment.

### **1.3 Emerging Macro Trends and Strategic Imperatives for 2025**

As the mobile market matures, several forward-looking trends are emerging that will define competitive advantage in 2025 and beyond. These trends reflect a strategic evolution driven by technological advancements and intensifying market pressures, forcing developers to innovate across their entire operational stack, from product development to user acquisition and monetization.

First, Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transitioning from a conceptual buzzword to a critical, operational pillar of the mobile app industry. In 2024, the focus was largely on generative AI for content creation; in 2025, the emphasis is shifting to more robust applications that drive core business metrics.1 In the fitness sector, AI is being used to deliver hyper-personalized workout and nutrition plans that adapt to user performance in real-time, significantly enhancing engagement and perceived value.9 In gaming, AI-powered models are being deployed to optimize user acquisition campaigns, predict lifetime value (LTV), and tailor retention events, thereby extending user engagement and maximizing revenue in an era of rising acquisition costs.2

Second, regulatory scrutiny and the high platform fees charged by Apple and Google are catalyzing the rise of alternative distribution channels. Direct-to-consumer (DTC) webshops and third-party app stores are gaining significant traction as publishers seek to bypass the standard 30% revenue share and establish a direct relationship with their users.11 This trend is particularly pronounced in the gaming sector, where major publishers are leveraging new regulations to direct players to their own web-based storefronts for in-app purchases. In 2024, 72% of top-performing games already operated a DTC webstore, a number expected to grow in 2025\.11

Finally, the adoption of hybrid monetization models has become a strategic default, particularly in the gaming vertical. The realization that a small fraction of users drives the majority of in-app purchase revenue has made it imperative to effectively monetize the non-paying majority through in-app advertising (IAA). Simultaneously, even ad-reliant genres are incorporating IAP and subscription elements to diversify revenue streams.12 This blending of IAP, IAA, and subscriptions is no longer a niche strategy but a core tenet of modern app monetization, essential for maximizing LTV across the entire user base.13

The confluence of these market forces creates a complex and challenging environment. While both consumer spending and advertising investment are at all-time highs, the tools available for precise user targeting and measurement have been constrained by privacy regulations. This has led to a significant increase in the cost and difficulty of acquiring high-value users.2 The result is a feedback loop where the challenges in advertising-funded user acquisition place a greater premium on direct monetization through IAPs and subscriptions. In turn, the high cost of acquiring these paying users necessitates a sophisticated ad monetization strategy to generate revenue from the vast non-paying user base. Therefore, to succeed in 2025, developers cannot simply choose one monetization path; they must achieve operational excellence across a hybrid model to remain competitive.

## **Section 2: Monetization Benchmarks for Mobile Gaming: The Core Experience**

### **2.1 Market Landscape for Core Gaming in North America**

The North American mobile gaming market represents the largest and most lucrative segment of the global gaming industry. In 2024, the total regional gaming market was valued at $68.5 billion, with a strong growth trajectory indicated by a projected compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 7.5% from 2025 to 2030\.16 Within this landscape, mobile gaming is the undisputed leader, accounting for 52.2% of all gaming revenue in 2024 and being the fastest-growing platform segment.2 The United States is the engine of this market, expected to generate $25 billion in mobile game revenue in 2025, making it the single most valuable country for mobile game developers.7

The Fighting Games sub-genre, the focus of this analysis, is a significant and growing component of this market. Mobile has become the dominant platform for this genre, commanding a 51.1% share of the fighting games market, which underscores the relevance of mobile-centric monetization benchmarks.18 The prevailing business model for mobile fighting games is freemium, which relies on a carefully balanced economy of in-game purchases (such as new characters, cosmetic skins, and progression boosters), in-app advertising, and recurring revenue models like season or battle passes.19

While granular, publicly available monetization data specifically for the "Fighting Games" category is limited, the genre's core attributes provide a clear pathway for analysis. Its emphasis on skill-based progression, competitive multiplayer loops, deep customization, and live-service content updates aligns it perfectly with the broader "Mid-core" and "Action" game categories. These genres are defined by their blend of accessible mechanics and deep, long-term engagement systems. Therefore, utilizing benchmarks from the Mid-core and Action genres offers the most accurate and relevant proxy for establishing performance expectations and strategic goals for a mobile fighting game in the North American market.20

### **2.2 Advertising Monetization Benchmarks (eCPM)**

For any freemium mobile game, advertising revenue is a critical component of the overall monetization strategy. It serves the dual purpose of generating income from the vast majority of users who do not make in-app purchases and funding the user acquisition campaigns necessary for growth. The key metric for measuring the effectiveness of ad placements is the effective Cost Per Mille (eCPM), which represents the revenue earned per 1,000 ad impressions. In North America, eCPM values are among the highest in the world, but they vary significantly based on the ad format and the user's operating system.

Rewarded Video advertising consistently delivers the highest eCPMs, making it the most valuable ad format for game developers. These opt-in placements, which offer players an in-game reward (e.g., currency, extra lives, or item boosts) in exchange for watching a full video ad, are highly valued by advertisers due to their guaranteed engagement and positive user sentiment.23 Interstitial ads, which appear at natural transition points in the game (e.g., between levels), and App Open ads, which are displayed when a user launches the app, also provide strong revenue but at a lower rate than Rewarded Video. Banner ads, while useful for constant presence, generate the lowest eCPMs.23

A significant disparity in eCPM exists between iOS and Android. Advertisers are consistently willing to pay a premium to reach the iOS user base, resulting in higher eCPMs across all ad formats on Apple devices compared to their Android counterparts.23 This platform-based variance is a crucial factor for developers when forecasting revenue and modeling the lifetime value of their users.

### **2.3 User Purchase Behavior Benchmarks (Conversion, ARPU, ARPPU)**

While advertising monetizes the broad user base, the financial success of most Mid-core and Fighting games hinges on their ability to convert a small segment of players into paying users through in-app purchases (IAP). This economic model, often referred to as the "whale economy," is characterized by extremely low conversion rates but very high spending from those who do convert. Understanding the benchmarks for this user purchase funnel is essential for building a sustainable IAP-driven revenue stream.

The freemium-to-paid conversion rate in mobile gaming is exceptionally low. While general industry benchmarks for freemium apps range from 1% to 10%, the reality for gaming is on the far low end of that spectrum.24 A 2024 analysis from Unity revealed that a mere **1.83%** of mobile game players make any in-app purchases at all.15 This single data point is foundational to the entire monetization strategy of a core game; it confirms that the business is not built on monetizing the average player, but on identifying, nurturing, and retaining the tiny fraction of users who are willing to spend.

The **Average Revenue Per User (ARPU)** provides a holistic view of the game's economy by blending revenue from both paying and non-paying users. Recent Q3 2024 data for Mid-core games in Tier-1 Western markets, including North America, offers precise benchmarks for the first 90 days of a user's lifecycle (D90 ARPU).21 This data clearly demonstrates the superiority of a hybrid monetization model. On iOS, a hybrid approach yields a D90 ARPU of $9.69, outperforming an IAP-only model ($7.31). The effect is even more pronounced on Android, where hybrid models deliver significantly higher returns. On an annualized basis, the high spending power of the region is evident, with the average North American mobile gamer generating **$112 per year**, far surpassing the global average.17

Isolating the spending of the converted 1.83% of users reveals their immense value through the **Average Revenue Per Paying User (ARPPU)**. For Mid-core games, which include the Fighting genre, a typical monthly ARPPU falls within the range of **$25 to $50**. For more complex Hardcore games, this figure can rise to **$50 to $100 or more** per month.26 These figures highlight that a single paying user can be worth hundreds or even thousands of times more than a non-paying user over their lifetime.

### **2.4 Strategic Recommendations for Gaming Monetization**

The benchmark data for the North American mobile gaming market points toward several clear strategic imperatives for developers of Fighting games and other Mid-core titles in 2025\.

First, the data reveals a fundamental divergence in optimal monetization strategy between the two major platforms. For iOS, where D90 ARPU is high for both IAP-only ($7.31) and hybrid ($9.69) models, a strategy heavily focused on a premium IAP experience is highly viable.21 While a hybrid model provides an uplift, the core of the iOS business can be built around direct user spending. In contrast, the data for Android suggests that a hybrid monetization model is not merely an option but an essential requirement for maximizing revenue. The average Android user appears more receptive to an ad-based value exchange, and failing to integrate rewarded video ads deeply into the core game loop means leaving significant revenue unrealized. A game's economy cannot simply be ported from iOS to Android; the Android version must be designed from the ground up as a hybrid-first experience to be competitive.

Second, the entire business model must be architected around "whale hunting" and retention. With a conversion rate below 2% and a monthly ARPPU potentially exceeding $50, the overwhelming majority of IAP revenue will come from a very small and dedicated group of players.15 This necessitates a two-pronged approach. The user acquisition strategy must move beyond simple install volume and focus on event-based optimizations designed to find users who exhibit early signs of high spending potential.13 Concurrently, the live operations (live-ops) and product strategy must be built to cater to these high-value players, incorporating VIP programs, exclusive offers, and personalized content to maximize their long-term engagement and lifetime value.27

Finally, for the Fighting Game genre specifically, the primary driver of engagement and monetization is a robust live-ops calendar. The market is defined by a continuous cycle of live events, the introduction of new characters and cosmetic items, and the sale of season passes that provide access to this new content.19 This creates a predictable and recurring cadence of spending opportunities that keeps the core player base engaged and invested. This live-service model is the principal lever for driving repeat purchases and is the most effective way to monetize the passionate, competitive community that defines the genre.

## **Section 3: Monetization Benchmarks for Mobile Fitness Apps: The Subscription Economy**

### **3.1 Market Landscape for Subscription Fitness Apps in North America**

The mobile fitness application vertical in North America represents a mature, high-value market characterized by strong consumer intent and a dominant subscription-based business model. The region is the global epicenter for this category, accounting for a commanding **46.61%** of worldwide fitness app revenue in 2024\.6 This market concentration translates into substantial financial scale; the United States market alone is projected to generate **$2.49 billion** in revenue in 2025, with continued growth expected in the subsequent years.3

This financial strength is built on a foundation of high user adoption and a willingness to pay for premium services. An estimated 74% of Americans use at least one fitness app, indicating deep market penetration.6 Unlike the broad-funnel approach often seen in gaming, users who download a fitness app typically do so with a specific, real-world goal in mind, such as weight loss, strength training, or wellness tracking. This high-intent user base is more predisposed to paying for a service that promises to help them achieve these goals. This is reflected in the platform revenue split, where iOS, with its higher-spending user base, contributes 52.13% of all fitness app revenue, despite having a smaller market share than Android.6 This dynamic makes North America the prime market for developers of subscription-based fitness services.

### **3.2 The Subscription Conversion Funnel**

The journey of a fitness app user from initial download to becoming a paying subscriber can be precisely measured through a series of conversion funnel benchmarks. These metrics are starkly different from those in the gaming vertical and highlight the unique, high-intent nature of the fitness app user base.

The most telling metric is the **download-to-paid conversion rate**. For Health & Fitness apps in North America, the median rate at which a new user converts to a paying subscriber within their first 30 days is an exceptionally high **9.4%**.30 This figure is more than five times the typical IAP conversion rate seen in mobile games, immediately signaling that a significant portion of the user base downloads these apps with the explicit intention of potentially paying for a service.

This conversion process is most often facilitated through a free trial period, which serves as the critical evaluation phase for the user. The benchmarks for this trial funnel are equally strong. The median **install-to-trial start rate** for fitness apps is **6.9%**, with the top 25% of apps convincing up to 13.5% of their new users to begin a trial.30 Once a user has committed to starting a trial, the likelihood of them converting to a paid subscription is very high. The median **trial-to-paid conversion rate** is **44.5%**, meaning nearly half of all trial users become paying subscribers. For top-performing apps in the upper quartile, this rate climbs to an impressive **58.8%**.30 This data confirms that the trial experience is the single most important phase in the user journey; a compelling and value-driven trial is the gateway to successful monetization.

### **3.3 Subscriber Value and Revenue Benchmarks (ARPU & LTV)**

Quantifying the financial value of a fitness app user is accomplished through two key sets of metrics: Average Revenue Per User (ARPU), which measures the revenue generated from all installs, and Payer Lifetime Value (LTV), which isolates the total revenue generated from a paying subscriber.

**ARPU**, often measured as Revenue Per Install (RPI) in the subscription context, demonstrates how quickly an app begins to generate revenue from a new cohort of users. For Health & Fitness apps, the median 14-day ARPU is **$0.44**, rising to **$0.63** after 60 days.31 While these early figures may seem modest, they accumulate into a significant annualized figure. The projected overall ARPU for the fitness app market in 2025 is **$20.79**, which accounts for revenue spread across the entire user base over a full year.3

The most critical metric for any subscription business is **Payer LTV**, as it defines the total worth of a converted customer and thus dictates the allowable customer acquisition cost (CAC). For fitness apps, the LTV figures are robust. The median **realized LTV after one month** for a paying subscriber is **$16.44**. For the top 25% of apps, this figure nearly doubles to **$31.12**, indicating that the best-in-class apps are exceptionally effective at retaining their subscribers beyond the first payment.31 When looking at longer-term value, particularly for apps that successfully convert users to annual plans, the median **12-month realized LTV** is **$46.10**.33 This demonstrates the substantial, recurring revenue stream that a single loyal subscriber can provide.

### **3.4 Strategic Recommendations for Fitness App Monetization**

The benchmark data for the North American fitness app market reveals a clear, front-loaded monetization funnel that dictates a specific set of strategic priorities for 2025\.

First, the entire user acquisition and onboarding strategy must be **optimized for high intent**. With a 9.4% download-to-paid conversion rate, it is evident that a large portion of users are arriving with a pre-existing motivation to solve a problem.30 Marketing efforts should therefore focus on channels and messaging that capture this intent, rather than on broad, awareness-based campaigns. The First Time User Experience (FTUE) should be designed to rapidly identify the user's specific goal and immediately demonstrate how the app provides a clear, effective path to achieving it.

Second, developers must **master the trial period**. With a trial-to-paid conversion rate of nearly 45%, the free trial is the definitive "moment of truth" for a fitness app.30 This period is where the app's value proposition is validated or rejected. Consequently, significant resources should be allocated to A/B testing every aspect of the trial experience. This includes optimizing the trial length (data suggests 5-9 days is becoming the industry standard), refining the onboarding flow to ensure users engage with key features, and determining the optimal level of feature access to showcase value without cannibalizing the incentive to upgrade.31

Finally, the pricing and packaging strategy must **strategically balance annual and monthly plans**. Data shows that annual subscriptions are the primary driver of high LTV in the fitness category, with 67% of all subscriptions being yearly plans.31 The marketing and paywall design should aggressively merchandise the superior value of the annual plan, often by highlighting a significant discount (e.g., "50% off") compared to the monthly price. However, this must be paired with a robust early engagement and support strategy. A large, upfront annual payment can lead to a higher risk of buyer's remorse and refund requests if the user does not quickly form a habit with the app.33 Therefore, post-conversion onboarding, push notification strategies, and community features are critical for reinforcing the value of that long-term commitment.

## **Section 4: Synthesis and Forward-Looking Outlook**

### **4.1 Comparative Analysis: Divergent Monetization Philosophies**

The analysis of monetization benchmarks for Mobile Gaming and Mobile Fitness apps in North America reveals two highly lucrative but fundamentally divergent business models. Their success is predicated on entirely different user psychologies, economic structures, and strategic imperatives. Understanding this philosophical divide is crucial for any developer, operator, or investor in the mobile app space.

Monetization in the **Core/Fighting Game** vertical is based on a **"created economy."** The value proposition is entirely internal to the game world. Users spend real money on virtual assets—such as characters, cosmetic skins, or in-game currency—that have no utility outside of the entertainment experience. Success in this model depends on the developer's ability to create deep, long-term engagement loops that keep players invested for months or years. The economic engine is driven by a continuous stream of new content and compelling spending opportunities designed to appeal to a very small fraction of the user base. It is a low-conversion (under 2%), high-ARPPU, whale-driven model where the lifetime value of a single paying user can be orders of magnitude greater than that of a non-payer.

Conversely, monetization in the **Subscription Fitness** vertical is based on delivering **"real-world outcomes."** The value proposition is external; the app is a tool to help users achieve tangible, personal goals like losing weight, building muscle, or improving overall wellness. Success depends on the ability to quickly and effectively demonstrate this utility to a high-intent user base. The economic engine is driven by converting a significant percentage of users (over 9%) to a recurring payment model based on the promise of achieving their desired results. It is a high-conversion, high-intent, subscription-driven model where the primary challenge is not converting a user to their first payment, but retaining them after their initial goal is met.

### **4.2 Key Takeaways for Developers and Investors**

The distinct nature of these two verticals translates into different sets of key takeaways for stakeholders.

**For Gaming Developers and Investors:** Success in the North American market in 2025 requires a dual mastery of two complex domains. First is the science of sophisticated, data-driven user acquisition aimed at identifying and acquiring potential high-spenders or "whales." This involves moving beyond simple cost-per-install campaigns to embrace LTV-prediction models and event-based optimizations. Second is the art of designing a robust hybrid monetization system. This system must cater to the high-spending whales with compelling IAP offers while simultaneously extracting value from the 98% of non-paying users through seamlessly integrated rewarded advertising. The data strongly indicates that for the Android platform, this hybrid approach is not just an optimization but a strategic necessity.

**For Fitness App Developers and Investors:** Success in the North American market hinges on a different set of capabilities. The primary focus must be on capturing high-intent users at the moment of need and converting them through a frictionless and highly effective trial experience. The business model is less about creating long-term engagement loops for their own sake and more about delivering demonstrable, real-world value that justifies a premium, recurring subscription. The key to maximizing lifetime value lies in successfully upselling users to an annual plan and then providing enough ongoing value—through new content, community, and progress tracking—to ensure they renew that commitment.

### **4.3 Future Outlook and Concluding Remarks**

Looking ahead through 2025, the macro trends identified in this report—AI-driven personalization, the diversification of monetization models, and the exploration of new distribution channels—will continue to accelerate and shape both verticals.

For the gaming sector, the primary challenge will be navigating the escalating costs of user acquisition in a privacy-constrained world. Competitive advantage will increasingly belong to studios that can most accurately predict LTV early in a user's lifecycle and that can build deep, engaging live-service ecosystems that retain their core spender base for years.

For the fitness sector, the main challenge will be defending premium subscription price points against a backdrop of increasing competition and the risk of goal-based churn. As the market becomes more saturated, the ability to prove long-term, ongoing value beyond a user's initial fitness goal will be the key differentiator that separates market leaders from the rest.

In conclusion, while both the mobile gaming and mobile fitness markets in North America offer immense financial opportunities, they are not interchangeable. They represent fundamentally different business propositions that demand distinct strategies, team capabilities, and metrics for success. Stakeholders must deeply understand the unique economic and psychological drivers of their chosen vertical to build a sustainable and profitable enterprise in the dynamic mobile landscape of 2025\.
